WIL

Here I critically reflect on my actions and what I learnt during/ from the WIL placement.

Knowing when to be independent

My placement supervisor instructed me to read one academic paper to understand the problem context and explore interval arithmetic using Python. Initially, I felt motivated and responsible for structuring my learning process as no detailed content structure was provided, unlike in other courses. I self-studied algorithms, delved into mathematics and read academic papers related to my placement topic. I felt overwhelmed and incompetent as my existing knowledge and skills were inadequate to understand parts of the project. However, after talking with my placement supervisor, I understood that it is natural to lack some technical skills and knowledge at the commencement of a job. While learning new things will always be a part of any new job, I have learnt the necessity of focusing on essential points with sufficient depth and breadth without delving deep into everything. To improve my ability to plan learning tasks without going off-course, I will practice using notetaking and task management software like Notion to list the skills and knowledge needed, followed by a course of action to complete these tasks. This way, I can have an overview of what I have learnt and the remaining gaps in knowledge that needs addressing without getting side-tracked.

When I did not understand the method to apply interval arithmetic, I kept on researching on the Internet to find answers. I felt obligated to be an independent learner, who must decipher and solve problems with minimal guidance. I learnt that I had a misconception ‚Äì that asking for assistance undermines my ability to conduct tasks assigned to me and makes me reliant. My reluctance to ask my supervisor questions due to a desire to be independent hindered the progress of my placement by several weeks. When I finally communicated my struggles to my placement supervisor, his response made me realise that mentors are willing to assist and share their knowledge. Moving forward, as I start my career in IT, I shall ask teammates and supervisors questions, when necessary, instead of ‚Äúsuffering silently‚Äù and wasting time on futile attempts. In any workplace, I will face strict deadlines to produce deliverables to circumvent financial penalties. Hence, I must not hesitate to seek help merely because I wish to prove my independence. I have learnt that there must be a balance between communicating my struggles to someone who can assist me, with being proactive in learning on my own. Additionally, knowing how to communicate these needs is a crucial skill, as asking vague questions will cause miscommunication.

Niche field problems

The most surprising insight I gained is how important it is to learn from field experts about topics less explored. I have long held the belief that in the field of IT, everything there is to know is well-documented on the Internet and a simple Google search will give me an influx of relevant information. When I searched for Interval Arithmetic libraries and their applications, I did find several options. However, as I explored these libraries, it became evident to me that some of them are underdeveloped, not maintained or irrelevant, given how uncommon it is to use in coding in the context of my placement goals. This challenged my assumptions and made me aware of my reliance on the Internet as the only source of knowledge. My supervisor suggested I explore PyIbex (python library) which did not appear in the top 10 pages of my Google search results. I was surprised that PyIbex did not appear as a top search, but my supervisor was aware of its existence and relevance. I learnt that years of experience and exposure gave him knowledge and insight that I cannot gain by doing simple Internet searches and reading. This realisation opened my eyes to the option of using my supervisor‚Äôs experience as a crucial source of relevant information, as self-exploration on the Internet will not always provide me with the exact information I need in a reasonable timeframe. In future jobs, I will ask field experts or experienced mentors appropriate questions to help speed up my learning process. During meetings with the supervisor, I listened to his explanations and did not know what questions to ask to maximise my time with him. After the meeting, I realised I did have questions but felt doubtful about the quality of my questions and thus, was hesitant to ask. I shall learn to plan for meetings with a list of questions and how to record information conveyed to me. I learnt that it is not a viable option to expect experts to spend time explaining in detail, so asking the right questions is a valuable skill I intend to polish. I intend to read books (e.g., ‚ÄúThe Art of Asking‚Äù by Terry Fadem) and articles on asking questions during meetings to gain this skill.

Technical & Transferable Skills

Here I reflect on the technical and transferable skills I developed and improved through the WIL placement

Algorithm Construction + Mathematics

I was offered the placement by Dr Yong Wu as I had experience with a relevant coding language (python), data analysis and machine learning. I was required to work on validating optimisation solution for a packing problem by using/creating an appropriate algorithm. I accepted the placement as I had an opportunity to practice these skills and learn additional technical skills. Prior to the placement, I had knowledge of some algorithm types, primarily sorting algorithms. The algorithm needed in this placement was a search algorithm. My placement supervisor told me to learn about Branch and Bound algorithm (B&B) as it is most relevant. While researching on the uses of B&B, I got exposure to dynamic algorithms. I learnt that B&B is a merger of search and dynamic algorithm. Overall, my knowledge of algorithm types and uses has increased exponentially through reading and researching.

When my placement supervisor told me to use B&B in the solution, my approach was to find an existing B&B algorithm I could implement on the packing problem I was given. I spent considerable time searching existing libraries and attempting to implement it. I then learnt that most of these B&B algorithms are created for very specific use cases, unlike general-use algorithms (e.g., Support Vector Machine) that I had experience using. The skills I had to apply were an extension of my prior knowledge, but very different in execution. Previously, I had taken general-function algorithm codes from existing libraries like Scikit-learn and implemented them on large datasets. This meant a writing a few lines of codes, with the focus being on processing data and interpretation of results. The aim would be to obtain insights on existing data. However, this placement needed me to create such an algorithm and create data to test the algorithm. While both situations dealt with algorithms and data, I found the process of creating to be exponentially more complex than implementing. I should have focused on learning about how algorithms are built, instead of attempting to carry out the same techniques I had done in other courses.

The process of simulating the packing problem and creating dataset was yet another challenge. I had to understand the properties of circles and create mathematical equations to describe the packing process. Data analytics does require various branches of mathematics (e.g., statistics) in algorithms and discovering insights from data. The improvement in mathematical literacy and algorithm use will make me a better data analyst.

Coding skills: Python

While algorithm-use and python programming are intertwined, since algorithms can be written in Python language, the skills I needed for each is quite different. To be able to implement the best algorithm, the focus is on the results of implementation on datasets, that translates to useful insights. The writing of codes to implement algorithm is significantly simplified through detailed codes description provided in libraries. Coding in this context required me to be focused on the language syntax (e.g., debugging when I had errors). To be able to write programs well, I needed to be methodological and translate logical processes (i.e., optimising packing circles problem) into lines of code with correct syntax. I realised I could be good at implementing algorithms without being as good at coding. However, coding skills will make implementing algorithms an effortless process. This challenged me as the one of the key tasks was to write codes for a program.

I proceeded to look at over 300 lines of code written for a specific implementation of B&B algorithm. This was the most eye-opening experience ‚Äì I did not once think about the complexity in coding logic of an algorithm, despite having used numerous algorithms. While I had experience coding programs, they were relatively simple tasks with singular functionality (e.g., a program to calculate area based on user input). The logic of algorithms considers numerous calculations and situations. I obtained a skeletal of what my B&B coding logic should contain by analysing the codes. Additionally, I had to re-read Python class methods to understand and use it. Initially I found coding challenging, but as I practiced writing, the syntax became more intuitive. While I have become more comfortable and faster in coding with Python, I recognise that more practice is needed to master it. To gain confidence and versatility in my coding skills, I shall practice coding to solve python problems (e.g., LeetCode problems).

Data analysis is the marriage of coding/using algorithms, creating visualisations, handling data, numeric literacy and possessing domain knowledge. In the placement, I had to create an algorithm, create visualisations, fabricate testing data, understand interval arithmetic (mathematics) and lastly, understand the packing problem (domain knowledge) to optimise it. Although I had not successfully created the optimization algorithm, I had a chance to practise many skills, which is the first step to being an excellent data analyst.
